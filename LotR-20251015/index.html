Assault on Rivendell


This is a scenario of Games Workshop's Middle Earth Strategy Battle Game, from the Rise of Angmar sourcebook. Here we see the army of Angmar attack the Elven haven of Rivendell. Elrond leads a force which must hold off the forces of Evil until reinforcements can arrive to save the day.

The scenario lasts twelve turns. Evil wins if they can reduce the Good side to six or less models before the end of the game. Otherwise Good wins.

(Click on any image to see an enlargement.)

The Forces


The Good side has 25 Rivendell elves (8 with spear & shield, 8 with bow, 8 with elven sword, and one with a banner) led by Elrond and Erestor.

The Evil forces are mix: 8 orcs with shield, 8 with spear, 4 with bow, 4 with two-handed weapon, 1 with banner, led by 2 Orc Captains. These troops are reinforced by 6 Warg Riders (2 with hand weapon, 2 with spear, 2 with bow) and 12 Warriors of Carn Dûm (6 with spear, 6 with hand weapon) led by a Captain of Carn Dûm.

GW's been pretty good recently about making their scenario orders of battle match the packaging of the troops. For example, the orcs here are the exact distribution you get from two of GW's orc sprues. But the Warriors of Carn Dûm are sold in groups of 6, one of which can optionally be configured as a captain. So to play this scenario with no substitutions, you'd need to buy three sets of Warriors of Carn Dûm and you'd then have five leftovers. It's unfortunate. I suspect originally GW planned to have a separate command pack and then changed their mind for whatever reason.

That rankles, so for purposes of this scenario, I decided instead to use Dunland's Frida Tallspear as my Captain of Carn Dûm figure.

Setup


The board is the usual 4'x4', with the houses representing the outskirts of Rivendell. Good sets up first within 12" of the south edge. The bowmen set up in the middle, backed by the leaders and the bannerelf. Each flank gets a squad of swordselves backed by spearelves.

With the elves having higher Fight values across the board, Evil needs numerical advantage to win combats. So he decides to virtually abandon his right flank, posting just 4 archers there. With luck, maybe they'll kill an elf or two. The bulk of the orcs are arrayed in the center, while the main effort goes against Good's right flank.

Turn 1


Before the game starts, Elrond rolls for his Foresight points, which can be used to alter Good's Priority Roll, and gets a disappointing 2.

Good begins narrative scenarios with Priority. Given that every turn without Combat helps the elves, Elrond pulls back his right flank (3rd image). The elven archers stay put in hopes of decimating the orcs (2nd image), while the squad on the left advances, in hopes of wiping out the Evil archers and flanking the orcs.

The boworcs stay put to fire, but the rest of the Evil force advances.

There's a lot of Shooting this turn, but it's almost all ineffective, with just a single orc slain (1st image).

Post-game Thoughts

By mid-game it looked like the elves were going to stroll to victory, but after that things got tense and the game really came down to the wire.

I didn't track luck explicitly, but the elves proved much hardier than I expected. Evil has no troops that can match the elves' Fight Value, so Good was winning a lot of tied Duel rolls. Elrond is especially tough, getting 3 Attacks with a Lord of the West re-roll plus a banner re-roll.

Running away to avoid fighting isn't particularly glorious or heroic, but it does seem like the best strategy for the elves. In which case Evil needs to attack even at poor odds in hopes that the elves will roll low.

The Priority rolls were extremely in favor of Good this game, despite the low roll for Elrond's Foresight. It would be interesting to see what would happen with more evenly-divided Priority.

Some of my elf models are among the first figures I painted. I'm glad to have finally gotten them on the table after 17(!) years.

My rating: ★★★★☆ Solid.
